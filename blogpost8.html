The Rise of India’s Small Satellite Launchers: SSLV, Vikram, and Agnibaan

India's space industry is witnessing a significant transformation, particularly with the development of small satellite launchers. As global demand for launching small satellites grows rapidly, organizations like ISRO (Indian Space Research Organisation) and private players such as Skyroot Aerospace and Agnikul Cosmos are at the forefront of this revolution. At the heart of this movement are three cutting-edge launch vehicles designed to cater to this growing market: ISRO's Small Satellite Launch Vehicle (SSLV), Skyroot's Vikram, and Agnikul Cosmos' Agnibaan.


The Emergence of the Small Satellite Market

Over the past decade, the space industry has seen a significant rise in demand for small satellites, particularly in areas like communications, Earth observation, climate monitoring, and Internet of Things (IoT) applications. Small satellites, also known as CubeSats or nanosatellites, are lightweight, cost-effective, and easier to deploy. This trend has encouraged space organizations worldwide to develop launch vehicles tailored to meet the specific needs of launching these smaller payloads into orbit.

Traditionally, launching small satellites required sharing space on larger rockets designed for heavy payloads. While effective, this approach often meant longer wait times and less flexibility in deployment schedules. The development of dedicated small satellite launch vehicles offers a more streamlined and responsive solution, enabling quicker access to space at reduced costs. India, with its vast experience in space technology and growing private sector, is positioning itself as a key player in this domain.


ISRO’s Small Satellite Launch Vehicle (SSLV)

The SSLV is ISRO’s response to the rapidly growing demand for launching small satellites into space. The vehicle is designed to provide on-demand access to space, particularly for commercial and academic entities looking to deploy satellites weighing between 10 kg and 500 kg into Low Earth Orbit (LEO). Unlike the larger Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) and Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle (GSLV), which are typically used for launching heavy payloads, the SSLV is a more compact and cost-effective solution. It is engineered for quick turnaround times, meaning that it can be readied for launch with minimal setup, allowing multiple launches per year. The goal is to make satellite deployment as accessible and flexible as possible for commercial and government clients.


Key features of SSLV:


Quick Turnaround: The SSLV requires only a few days to be integrated and launched, significantly reducing the preparation time compared to larger launch vehicles.


Cost-Effective: With reduced setup costs, SSLV offers a more economical solution, particularly attractive to smaller companies and academic institutions.


Payload Flexibility: It can carry multiple small satellites simultaneously, making it ideal for constellation deployments, where a series of satellites need to be placed in orbit.


Skyroot Aerospace and the Vikram Series

Skyroot Aerospace, a Hyderabad-based private space startup, is one of the most promising players in India’s burgeoning space sector. The company, founded by former ISRO engineers, aims to revolutionize space access by building affordable and reliable launch vehicles. Their flagship rocket series, Vikram, is named after Dr. Vikram Sarabhai, the father of the Indian space program. The Vikram Series rockets are designed for the small satellite market, offering payload capacities ranging from 225 kg to 720 kg, depending on the variant (Vikram I, II, or III). The rockets are engineered to be fully customizable to meet different mission requirements, giving them a competitive edge in the global space market.


Notable features of Vikram rockets include:


3D-Printed Components: Skyroot employs advanced 3D printing technology to manufacture certain rocket components, which reduces costs and increases efficiency.


Modular Design: Vikram rockets are modular, meaning that different stages and configurations can be adapted depending on the payload and mission objectives.


Private Space Innovation: As a private player, Skyroot is at the forefront of India’s push to involve more private companies in space technology, reflecting a shift in India’s space policy towards privatization.
